G.A.P.

Ground. Attune. Proceed.

One practice, three entries — for the moments life demands a decision, and the moments life simply asks to be met. Each word below opens into something you can do right now, drawn from contemplative traditions and grounded in how the nervous system actually settles.

the practice

G.A.P. is not a technique to master once. It is a small door you can walk through many times a day — before a hard conversation, in the middle of a spiral, or simply to come back to yourself.

Unhooking from thought and emotion. A sense of being where you are — before anything is decided or acted on.

The Three-Part Breath

Press begin when ready

0 of 5 rounds

  • 1 Two inhales through the nose — the first fills the belly and chest, the second tops you off completely. No pause between them.
  • 2 One long, open-mouth exhale — let the breath fall out entirely, with a sigh if it wants one.
  • 3 Repeat for five to eight rounds, or until something in the body visibly settles.

The double inhale is borrowed from the "physiological sigh," shown to lower arousal faster than a single slow breath by more fully re-inflating collapsed lung tissue before the release.

The Felt-Sense Check

1 of 5

Adapted from Eugene Gendlin's Focusing method — asking the body for a "felt sense" of a decision before the mind argues for it. Studied for decades as a way of accessing implicit, pre-verbal knowing.

application

As a decision compass

Ground with the breath. Attune to what the moment, the body, and the situation are showing you. Proceed into the choice from that settled place.

As a daily practice

Ground in the body and the space around you. Attune without naming what you find. Proceed by moving fluidly with life — not toward the habitual escape.

Building the skill of intentional connection and response in day-to-day life.

More Practice

Five ways to deepen what Ground, Attune, and Proceed already point toward.

A simple anchor-breath sit, the root practice underneath Ground.

  1. Sit comfortably. Let your eyes close or soften toward the floor.
  2. Pick one anchor — the breath at the nostrils, or the rise and fall of the belly.
  3. When the mind wanders, simply notice "thinking" and return to the anchor. This is the whole practice, not a failure of it.
  4. Begin with five minutes. Let the duration grow on its own.

This is the same attentional training studied in mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), with consistent evidence for reducing rumination and reactivity over time.

A slow pass through the body, deepening what Attune began.

  1. Lie down or sit reclined. Begin at the feet.
  2. Move attention slowly upward — feet, legs, pelvis, belly, chest, hands, arms, shoulders, neck, face.
  3. At each area, just notice what is there — warmth, tension, tingling, nothing at all. No need to change it.
  4. If tension is strong somewhere, you can breathe into it gently before moving on.

Alternative — body tapping: use fingertips to lightly tap down the arms, chest, and legs. This can be more accessible than stillness for a body that's activated or numbed out.

A second breath tool, distinct from the three-part breath — useful when you need steadiness more than release.

  1. Inhale through the nose for a count of four.
  2. Hold gently for a count of four.
  3. Exhale through the nose for a count of four.
  4. Hold empty for a count of four. Repeat for four to eight rounds.

Used widely in clinical anxiety protocols and by high-stress professions to down-regulate the nervous system through slow, even-paced breathing.

A quiet self-inquiry, for when a feeling or belief keeps returning.

  1. Name what's present in a single honest sentence — "I feel ___" or "I believe ___."
  2. Ask: is this absolutely true? Sit with the question rather than rushing to answer.
  3. Ask: who or how would I be without this thought?
  4. Let the inquiry stay open. Insight here tends to arrive sideways, not on command.

This draws on contemplative self-inquiry traditions and cognitive defusion work — loosening identification with a thought without needing to argue it away.

How Attune and Proceed become one continuous motion, rather than two separate steps.

  1. Set a few natural check-in points in your day — before opening your laptop, at a stoplight, before a meal.
  2. At each, take one physiological sigh and notice one thing in the body or the room.
  3. Let whatever you do next simply follow from that — no analysis required.
  4. Over time this collapses Ground, Attune, and Proceed into a single felt movement, rather than three distinct stops.

This is the long-run aim of the framework — not a technique you perform, but a way of meeting each moment that becomes more automatic with repetition.
